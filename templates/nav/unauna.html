Una-Una

An isolated, idyllic, volcanic island with thriving coral reefs and a welcoming local community

The Island

Una-Una lies in the centre of the Gulf of Tomini, Sulawesi, Indonesia and is part of the Togian Archipelago. The Tomini Bay is deep, sheltered and equatorial which makes it an excellent diving location with visibility regularly over 30 m.

Agricultural island

Agriculture is the main source of income for the Togian islands, with coconuts and cocoa coming in top with respects to percentage land use. Una-Una is a small, round island in which the inhabited parts are covered in coconut, cocoa and clove trees, some of its main exports. Go there at the right time and you can eat your weight in mangoes, let alone all the other tropical fruit trees dotted about if you know where to look or who to ask.

Local community

There are around 500 people living on Una-Una over the three main villages. The community is extremely warm and welcoming. Most families have a small boat to catch fish during sunset, making for immaculate views. Every Friday the main village hosts the island's football and volleyball matches.

Colo Eruption

The Colo Volcano erupted in 1983 and forced everybody off the island and over to Wakai, the nearby Togian island. Pyroclastic flows swept over the island which left it uninhabitable for a long time. The layers of ash that covered the island has created extremely fertile soil, which caused the initial population increase after the eruption.

The reefs

The surrounding reefs experienced a brief recovery period after the eruption which left the fish populations to thrive. The local small scale fishermen jumped on the opportunity to make the most of the exceptional fishing grounds. They are said to chase off boats coming from elsewhere, taking what they need for themselves and not choosing to export their catch.

The Diving

The diving around Una-Una is arguably some of the best in the Togians, especially with regards to fish abundance and diversity. Take for example the dive site Pinnacle I, otherwise known as Fish-O-Mania, where you can be surrounded by multiple schools of fish at once. Another spot in the south of the island, Apollo, gets regular visits from a tornado of barracuda comprised of what seems like thousands of fish. It is rare not to spot a Napoleon Wrasse on a dive because they haven't been targeted for sale to the international markets yet.

Dive sites

The 35 named dive sites regularly visited by the two local dive centres on the east side of the island provide a variety of habitats to explore the huge diversity of life around this little island, sitting centre left in the middle of the coral triangle. Whether you are diving the fringing reefs, deep walls or pinnacles around the island, Una-Una is known for its huge sponge and coral formations alongside excellent visibility and calm conditions. The few muk diving sites, though not comparable to nearby Lembeh, Gorontalo, Bangka or Bunaken, provide a wealth of critters such as nudi's, shirmps, crabs, sea/pipehorses and more if you know where to look.

Marine Life

Due to the relatively low fishing impact compared to surrounding areas, the bigger than average fish size makes for compelling dives, the oversized many-spotted sweetlips all over the place are a prime example of this. The larger sightings includes eagle rays, tuna, barracuda, groupers, snappers, turtles, octopus, cuttlefish, napoleon wrasse, bumphead parrotfish and giant trevallies. Also very rare sightings of whale sharks and dolphins if you are lucky!

Toby Swarms

Last but not least, multiple times a year Una-Una experiences population explosions of whitebelly tobys that almost completely cover the reefs and form huge clouds around you on a dive. It is currently the only documented place on earth where this happens. While the tobys are swarming the local fisherman can't catch any fish... perhaps because they are all feasting on the tobys - my personal theory for why the fish are larger than average around Una-Una.
